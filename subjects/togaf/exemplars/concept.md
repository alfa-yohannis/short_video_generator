# Stakeholders, Concerns and Views

A short tutorial on four foundational TOGAF ideas — Stakeholder, Concern, View, and
Viewpoint — and how they relate. This is the concept teaching method: define each
term as a titled card in plain words, show how they RELATE with labelled arrows,
and contrast the pair people most often confuse. These are exactly the kind of
technical terms ordinary people have never heard, so each one is explained simply
the first time it appears, with an everyday comparison. TOGAF is a methodology, not
program code, so every scene is built from labelled cards, boxes, and relationship
arrows, never source code, and never with an invented "official TOGAF icon" (TOGAF
has no standard icon set). The running example is from everyday life: a small
neighbourhood coffee shop that wants to start taking orders online.
Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT for term and relationship labels, HIGHLIGHT for the
term in focus, OK for a correct result, and DANGER for a common mistake. The
English version should be entirely in English, including the examples.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the official TOGAF definitions of Stakeholder,
Concern, View, and Viewpoint, and the exact way they relate, against an authoritative
source (The Open Group's TOGAF Standard) rather than recalling them from memory — so
the cards and arrows state the real definitions and the correct direction of each
relationship (for example, that a Viewpoint governs a View, not the other way round).

## The Idea (~18s)
Show "Stakeholders, Concerns and Views" as a LARGE CENTERED title with a short
subtitle beneath it, such as "Answering different people's questions". Say the
problem plainly: different people care about different things, and one giant
diagram cannot answer everyone at once — so TOGAF gives these four words to keep it
organized. Name the example: a small coffee shop adding online
ordering.

## Four Plain Definitions (~31s)
Lay out four titled cards, each ACCENT-labelled and explained in everyday words. A
Stakeholder is simply anyone who cares about the result — an owner, a worker, a
customer. A Concern is what that person worries about — cost, speed, safety. A View
is what one person actually sees, made to answer their worry — a chart, a plan, a
picture. A Viewpoint is the template and rules for making that kind of view — the
recipe, not the dish. HIGHLIGHT each card as it is defined.

## How They Relate (~31s)
Turn the cards into a relationship map of labelled boxes joined by ACCENT arrows
that read as plain words: a Stakeholder "has" a Concern; a View "answers" a
Concern; a Viewpoint "is the recipe for" a View. Build the chain link by link, each
new arrow in HIGHLIGHT, so it is clear which depends on which.

## View vs Viewpoint (~27s)
Contrast the two people mix up the most, as a simple side-by-side. A Viewpoint is
the reusable recipe — "how to draw a delivery-time chart" — and it is a DANGER
mistake to treat the recipe as the finished thing. A View is the actual chart you
end up with for one shop, shown in OK. The one-liner: the viewpoint is the lens,
the view is the photo you take through it.

## A Quick Example (~31s)
Ground it in the coffee shop. The Stakeholder is the shop manager. Their Concern is
"will online orders arrive on time?". The Viewpoint is a simple recipe that says a
delivery view must show order times and late deliveries. The View is the actual
coffee-shop delivery-time chart built from that recipe. Assemble the chain in OK,
the piece in play in HIGHLIGHT.

## Conclusion (~22s)
Recap in plain words: people (stakeholders) have worries (concerns); a view is a
picture made to answer a worry; and a viewpoint is the reusable recipe for that
picture. End with the line of sight on screen:
Stakeholder -> Concern -> View (made from a Viewpoint).
