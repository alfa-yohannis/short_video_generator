# ADM Overview

A short tutorial on the Architecture Development Method (ADM), the method at the
heart of the TOGAF framework. This is the adm_cycle teaching method: a process
topic, so its main visual is the ADM CYCLE — a clockwise ring of phases around a
central hub. TOGAF is full of technical terms, so every term is explained in plain
words the first time it appears — assume the viewer has never heard them. TOGAF is
a methodology, not program code, so every scene is built from clean labelled
diagrams — rounded boxes, the cycle, and arrows — never source code, and never
with an invented "official TOGAF icon" (TOGAF has no standard icon set). The ADM is
always drawn the canonical way: Phases A through H arranged clockwise on a ring with
Requirements Management as the central hub joined to every phase, the Preliminary
phase drawn outside the ring feeding one arrow into Phase A (Architecture Vision),
and Phase H looping back to Phase A to close the ring. The running example is from
everyday life: a small
neighbourhood coffee shop that wants to start taking orders online.
Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT for phase and relationship labels, HIGHLIGHT for the
phase in focus, OK for a finished result, and DANGER for a gap to be closed. The
English version should be entirely in English, including the examples.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the canonical ADM structure against an authoritative
TOGAF source (The Open Group's TOGAF Standard) instead of recalling it from memory, so
the diagram matches the official method. Confirm: the phase names and their clockwise
order; that the ring is Phases A through H only; that the Preliminary phase sits outside
the ring with a single arrow into Phase A (Architecture Vision) and is not a box on the
ring; that Requirements Management is the central hub joined to every phase; and that
Phase H (Architecture Change Management) loops back to Phase A to close the cycle. Also
confirm in plain words what each phase delivers.

## What It Is (~18s)
Show "ADM Overview" as a LARGE CENTERED title with a short subtitle beneath it,
such as "TOGAF's step-by-step recipe". Explain the words plainly: Enterprise
Architecture is just the big-picture plan of how a whole organization's business
and computer systems fit together — like a city plan, not a single building — and
the ADM is the step-by-step method TOGAF gives you to build and update that plan.
Name the example: a small coffee shop adding online ordering.

## The ADM Cycle (~34s)
Draw the cycle: a ring of labelled rounded boxes running clockwise — Phase A
Architecture Vision, Phase B Business Architecture, Phase C Information Systems
Architectures, Phase D Technology Architecture, Phase E Opportunities and Solutions,
Phase F Migration Planning, Phase G Implementation Governance, and Phase H
Architecture Change Management — joined by curved ACCENT arrows that show the
clockwise flow. Draw the Preliminary phase as a separate box outside the ring with
one arrow feeding into Phase A — it is the one-time setup before the cycle begins,
not a step on the ring. Close the ring with an arrow from Phase H back to Phase A.
Say it plainly: each box on the ring is one step, you go round in order, and the
centre keeps everyone's needs in view. Place Requirements Management as a hub in the
centre, joined to every phase. HIGHLIGHT each phase name in turn as it is introduced.

## What the Phases Deliver (~31s)
Group the phases by intent, each ACCENT-labelled, and explain each result in plain
words. Preliminary sets up the team and the ground rules. Phase A produces an
Architecture Vision — a short picture of what you want and why, agreed before you
start, like a sketch of a finished room before a renovation. Phases B, C, and D
describe the target for the business, the information and software, and the
technology underneath, and find the gaps. Phases E and F plan the work into an
Architecture Roadmap — simply the schedule of steps from today to the goal. Phases
G and H oversee delivery and handle later changes. Show each result in OK.

## Requirements at the Hub (~22s)
Bring the centre forward: Requirements Management — in plain words, keeping track
of everything the organization needs the architecture to do. Explain it is not a
step you finish but an always-on activity: every phase adds needs and reads needs,
so a change ripples through consistently. Draw two-way ACCENT arrows between the
hub and a couple of phases, and keep the hub in HIGHLIGHT.

## One Pass for a Coffee Shop (~39s)
Walk the coffee shop through a single lap of the cycle, the current phase in HIGHLIGHT.
Preliminary: agree the ground rules and set up the team. Phase A: the vision —
"take orders online and deliver within one year". Phase B: how the business will
work — a new way to take online orders and get them delivered. Phase C: the
software — an ordering app and a customer list. Phase D: the technology — renting
computing online (the cloud) and a way to take card payments. Phases E and F: a
roadmap of small steps. Phases G and H: keep the rollout on track and fold in what
is learned. Build it step by step, ending with the finished line of sight in OK.

## Conclusion (~25s)
Recap in plain words: the ADM is a repeating cycle — Phases A through H around a
Requirements Management hub, set up once by the Preliminary phase — that turns goals
into a checked, delivered design. Stress it is repeatable, and that you can go round again as
things change. End with the line of sight on screen:
Vision -> Target Design -> Roadmap -> Delivery.
