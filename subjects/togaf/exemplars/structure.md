# Enterprise Continuum

A short tutorial on the Enterprise Continuum, the part of the TOGAF framework that
sorts and stores its building parts. This is the structure teaching method: its
main visual is a SPECTRUM that runs from generic to specific, with real examples
placed onto it. "Enterprise Continuum" is a heavy term, so it and the others are
explained in plain words the first time they appear — assume the viewer has never
heard them. TOGAF is a methodology, not program code, so every scene is built from
labelled bands, boxes, and arrows, never source code, and never with an invented
"official TOGAF icon" (TOGAF has no standard icon set). The running example is from
everyday life: a small neighbourhood coffee shop that wants to start
taking orders online. Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT for band and relationship
labels, HIGHLIGHT for the item in focus, OK for a sensible placement, and DANGER
for a wrong one. The English version should be entirely in English, including the
examples.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the official structure of the Enterprise Continuum
against an authoritative source (The Open Group's TOGAF Standard) rather than
recalling it from memory: the names and order of its stages along the generic ->
specific spectrum (and the two continua it comprises), so the bands and the example
placements reflect the real taxonomy, not an invented one.

## What It Is (~18s)
Show "Enterprise Continuum" as a LARGE CENTERED title with a short subtitle beneath
it, such as "Sorting parts from general to specific". Say it plainly: it is just a
way to organize everything you build with — from broad, reusable ideas that fit
any business down to the one version tailored to your own shop — and a place to
keep them so you can reuse them later. Name the example: a small
coffee shop adding online ordering.

## The Spectrum (~32s)
Draw the signature visual: four labelled bands going from general to specific,
joined by an ACCENT arrow, each with a plain gloss. Foundation — basic ideas that
fit any organization. Common Systems — things many businesses share, like an online
shop. Industry — patterns common to one trade, like food ordering. Organization-
Specific — the one version built for your own shop. Note things get more tailored
as you move along, and more reusable as you move back.

## Two Shelves (~28s)
Split the spectrum into two aligned rows sharing the same general-to-specific line,
each ACCENT-labelled and glossed. The Architecture row holds the plans and reusable
parts (the "building blocks" — think LEGO bricks you assemble things from). The
Solutions row holds the real, working products that put those plans into practice.
Say plainly: a plan on the top shelf maps to a real product on the bottom shelf.

## Placing the Parts (~40s)
Place the coffee shop's parts onto the spectrum, the current item in HIGHLIGHT. On the
plans row: a basic reference model at the foundation, a general "online ordering"
building block, a food-ordering pattern at industry level, and the coffee shop's own
ordering design at the specific end. On the products row alongside it: a ready-made
ordering app that is adjusted into the live coffee-shop app. Settle the sensible
placement in OK.

## Where They Are Kept (~25s)
Show that all this lives in the Architecture Repository — explained plainly as the
shared cupboard where the parts are stored and found again — drawn as a labelled
structure: the big-picture map, the list of agreed standards, the library of
reference material, and the logbook of decisions. Note this is what lets the next
project reuse instead of starting over.

## Conclusion (~22s)
Recap in plain words: sort your parts from general to specific across a plans row
and a products row, and keep them in a shared cupboard so you reuse them. End with
the line of sight on screen:
Foundation -> Common -> Industry -> Your Own Shop.
