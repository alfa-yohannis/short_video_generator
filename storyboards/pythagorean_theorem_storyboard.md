# Pythagorean Theorem — Bilingual Tutorial

A short tutorial that introduces the Pythagorean Theorem visually: what it
states, why it holds (via a rearrangement proof), and how it shows up in
everyday measurement problems.

The same scene sources render to both landscape and portrait, so the video
doubles as long-form material and as Reels / Shorts content. Voices are male
in both languages. Narration text for every scene, and the Manim scene
sources themselves, are generated by the configured `ai_cli` from the
descriptions below — the storyboard is intentionally narration-free.

## Scene: 01_pengantar / Pengantar

**file:** scene_01_pengantar.py
**fallback_duration:** 16

### description
Title card. Introduce the theorem by name, hint at the right-triangle setup,
and frame the learning goal: by the end of the video, the viewer should be
able to state the theorem and read a simple geometric proof.

## Scene: 02_segitiga_siku_siku / SegitigaSikuSiku

**file:** scene_02_segitiga_siku_siku.py
**fallback_duration:** 18

### description
Build up a labelled right triangle on the blueprint background: the two legs
are `a` and `b`, the hypotenuse is `c`, and the right angle is marked. Stress
that the theorem only applies when one angle is exactly 90 degrees.

## Scene: 03_pernyataan / Pernyataan

**file:** scene_03_pernyataan.py
**fallback_duration:** 16

### description
Show the equation `a² + b² = c²` formally, then animate three squares — one
on each side of the triangle — so the algebraic statement matches the
geometric picture: the area of the square on the hypotenuse equals the sum
of the areas of the squares on the legs.

## Scene: 04_bukti_visual / BuktiVisual

**file:** scene_04_bukti_visual.py
**fallback_duration:** 28

### description
Visual proof by rearrangement. Show a large square of side `a + b` containing
four copies of the triangle, leaving the two leg-squares uncovered. Slide the
triangles into a new arrangement that instead leaves the hypotenuse-square
uncovered. Same outer square, same four triangles — therefore the uncovered
areas are equal. That is the theorem.

## Scene: 05_contoh / Contoh

**file:** scene_05_contoh.py
**fallback_duration:** 20

### description
Worked example. Given legs `a = 3` and `b = 4`, compute the hypotenuse step
by step: 9 + 16 = 25, so `c = 5`. Then show the classic 3-4-5 triangle as a
sanity check. Mention that the inverse direction also works: given any two
sides, the third can be found.

## Scene: 06_aplikasi_dan_kesimpulan / AplikasiDanKesimpulan

**file:** scene_06_aplikasi_dan_kesimpulan.py
**fallback_duration:** 22

### description
Wrap up with two short, concrete uses: finding the diagonal of a rectangular
screen, and finding the straight-line distance between two points on a map.
Close by reminding the viewer that the theorem hinges on one assumption —
the angle must be exactly ninety degrees.
