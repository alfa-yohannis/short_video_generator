# Phase A Architecture Vision

A short tutorial on Phase A, the Architecture Vision phase — the very first phase
of the Architecture Development Method (ADM), the step-by-step method at the heart
of the TOGAF framework. This is the adm_cycle teaching method: a phase topic, so
its main visual is the ADM CYCLE — a clockwise ring of phases around a central hub,
with this one phase put in focus and walked through its inputs, steps, and outputs.
TOGAF is full of technical terms, so every term is explained in plain words the
first time it appears — assume the viewer has never heard them. TOGAF is a
methodology, not program code, so every scene is built from clean labelled diagrams
— rounded boxes, the cycle, layered bands, and relationship arrows — never source
code, and never with an invented "official TOGAF icon" (TOGAF has no standard icon
set). The ADM is always drawn the canonical way: Phases A through H arranged
clockwise on a ring with Requirements Management as the central hub joined to every
phase, the Preliminary phase drawn outside the ring feeding one arrow into Phase A
(Architecture Vision), and Phase H looping back to Phase A to close the ring. The
running example is from everyday life: a small family bakery that wants to start
taking orders online. Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT for labels and relationship
arrows, HIGHLIGHT for the phase or part in focus, OK for an agreed or finished
result, and DANGER for a risk or gap. The English version should be entirely in
English, including the examples.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the canonical structure and terminology of Phase A
Architecture Vision against an authoritative TOGAF source (The Open Group's TOGAF
Standard) instead of recalling it from memory, so the diagram matches the official
method, not an invented one. Confirm the specific facts for this topic: that Phase A
is the first phase of the ADM cycle proper; its purpose (develop a high-level
aspirational vision and obtain agreement to proceed); its main inputs (a Request for
Architecture Work, business principles, goals and drivers, the organizational model,
the tailored architecture framework, and the existing Architecture Repository); its
key steps (establish the project, identify stakeholders and their concerns, confirm
goals and constraints, define scope, confirm architecture principles, develop the
Architecture Vision, define value and risks, and secure approval); and its key
outputs (an approved Statement of Architecture Work and the Architecture Vision
document). Because the ADM cycle is involved, also confirm: the phase names and their
clockwise order; that the ring is Phases A through H only; that the Preliminary phase
sits outside the ring with a single arrow into Phase A (Architecture Vision) and is
not a box on the ring; that Requirements Management is the central hub joined to
every phase; and that Phase H (Architecture Change Management) loops back to Phase A
to close the cycle. Confirm in plain words what each input and output means.

## What It Is (~18s)
Show "Phase A Architecture Vision" as a LARGE CENTERED title in PRIMARY with a short
subtitle beneath it, such as "Where the ADM cycle begins". Explain the words plainly:
Enterprise Architecture is just the big-picture plan of how a whole organization's
business and its computer systems fit together — like a city plan, not a single
building. The Architecture Vision is the short, agreed picture of what you want to
change and why, drawn before any detailed work starts — like a quick sketch of a
finished room before a renovation begins. Name the example: a small family bakery
that wants to start taking orders online.

## Where Phase A Sits in the ADM (~30s)
Draw the full ADM cycle so the viewer can locate this phase: a ring of labelled
rounded boxes running clockwise — Phase A Architecture Vision, Phase B Business
Architecture, Phase C Information Systems Architectures, Phase D Technology
Architecture, Phase E Opportunities and Solutions, Phase F Migration Planning,
Phase G Implementation Governance, and Phase H Architecture Change Management —
joined by curved ACCENT arrows showing the clockwise flow. Place Requirements
Management as the hub in the centre, joined to every phase; in plain words it just
means keeping track of everything the organization needs the plan to do. Draw the
Preliminary phase as a separate box outside the ring with one arrow feeding into
Phase A — it is the one-time setup before the cycle begins, not a step on the ring.
Close the ring with an arrow from Phase H back to Phase A. Put Phase A in HIGHLIGHT
and say it plainly: this is the first phase of the cycle, the doorway every lap
passes through.

## Why Phase A Exists (~22s)
Keep Phase A in HIGHLIGHT and state its purpose in plain words on titled ACCENT cards.
First card: agree where we are heading — produce a short, high-level Architecture
Vision that everyone signs up to before spending time on detail. Second card: agree
permission to proceed — produce a Statement of Architecture Work, which is simply a
short agreement that says what work will be done, by whom, and within what limits,
like the signed quote and scope you approve before builders start. Stress the idea:
Phase A is about getting agreement and direction early, so the later phases are not
wasted designing the wrong thing.

## Inputs, Steps, and Outputs (~28s)
Show Phase A opened up as three labelled bands flowing into each other with ACCENT
arrows. Band one, Inputs, each glossed plainly: a Request for Architecture Work (a
short note from a sponsor asking for the work to start), the business goals and
drivers (what the organization is trying to achieve and what is pushing it), and the
existing Architecture Repository (the shelf where past plans and reusable parts are
kept). Band two, Key Steps: identify the stakeholders (the people who care about or
are affected by the change) and their concerns; confirm the goals and any
constraints; define the scope (how much is in or out); confirm the architecture
principles (the agreed rules of thumb to design by); then draft the Architecture
Vision itself and name the risks. Band three, Outputs in OK: the Architecture Vision
document and the approved Statement of Architecture Work.

## What Phase A Hands On (~22s)
Show how Phase A connects to its neighbours with ACCENT relationship arrows. The
arrow from the Preliminary phase feeds Phase A its ground rules; Phase A then passes
its two OK deliverables — the Architecture Vision and the approved Statement of
Architecture Work — forward into Phase B Business Architecture, which works out how
the day-to-day business must change. Draw a two-way ACCENT arrow between Phase A and
the Requirements Management hub to show needs are recorded here and read by every
later phase. In plain words: Phase A does not build the detailed design — it sets the
target and the permission, and the boxes that follow fill in the detail.

## One Pass for a Family Bakery (~36s)
Walk the bakery through Phase A only, building each part step by step with the current
step in HIGHLIGHT. The Request for Architecture Work: the owner asks to start, saying
"we are losing weekend orders we cannot take by phone". Stakeholders and concerns:
the owner wants more sales, the counter staff want it simple to run, customers want
easy ordering. Goals and constraints: take online orders within six months on a small
budget. Scope: online ordering and pickup only — home delivery is out for now. The
Architecture Vision in OK: "customers order and pay online and collect in store
within six months". Name a risk in DANGER: staff may be overwhelmed at busy times.
Finish with the approved Statement of Architecture Work in OK — the short agreement
that this work may proceed within those limits.

## Conclusion (~22s)
Recap in plain words over a small ADM ring with Phase A in HIGHLIGHT: Phase A
Architecture Vision is the first phase of the cycle, fed by the Preliminary phase and
sitting around the Requirements Management hub. It turns a rough request into two
agreed things — a short Architecture Vision of where you are heading and an approved
Statement of Architecture Work giving permission to go on. Stress that getting
agreement early keeps the later phases from designing the wrong thing. End with the
line of sight on screen: Request -> Vision -> Agreed Scope -> Phase B.
