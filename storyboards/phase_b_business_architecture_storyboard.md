# Phase B Business Architecture

A short tutorial on Phase B, Business Architecture — the design step of TOGAF's
Architecture Development Method (ADM) where you plan how the organization actually
works. This is the adm_cycle teaching method: a phase topic, so its main visual is
the ADM CYCLE — a clockwise ring of Phases A through H around a central Requirements
Management hub, with Phase B put in focus. TOGAF is a methodology, not program code,
so every scene is built from clean labelled diagrams — rounded boxes, the cycle,
matrices, layered bands, spectrums, and relationship arrows — never source code, and
never with an invented "official TOGAF icon" (TOGAF has no standard icon set). TOGAF
is full of technical terms, so every TOGAF term is explained in plain words the first
time it appears — assume the viewer has never heard them. The ADM is always drawn the
canonical way: Phases A through H clockwise on a ring with Requirements Management as
the central hub joined to every phase, the Preliminary phase drawn outside the ring
feeding one arrow into Phase A (Architecture Vision), and Phase H looping back to
Phase A to close the ring. The running example is from everyday life: a small family
bakery that wants to start taking orders online. Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT for
phase and relationship labels, HIGHLIGHT for Phase B in focus, OK for a finished
result, and DANGER for a gap to be closed. The English version should be entirely in
English, including the examples.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the canonical structure and terminology of Phase B
Business Architecture against an authoritative TOGAF source (The Open Group's TOGAF
Standard) instead of recalling it from memory, so the diagram matches the official
method rather than an invented one. Confirm what Phase B actually does: its inputs
(the Architecture Vision and Statement of Architecture Work carried in from Phase A);
its ordered steps (select the viewpoints and reference models, describe the Baseline
Business Architecture, describe the Target Business Architecture, perform Gap
Analysis, define candidate roadmap components, and run the formal stakeholder review);
and its deliverables (the Target Business Architecture, the Gap Analysis results, the
Business Architecture parts of the Architecture Roadmap, and the updated Architecture
Definition Document). Confirm the building-block types Business Architecture uses —
business capabilities, value streams, the organization structure, and business
services, functions, and processes. Whenever the ADM cycle is drawn, confirm the ring
is Phases A through H only; that the Preliminary phase sits outside the ring with a
single arrow into Phase A (Architecture Vision) and is not a box on the ring; that
Requirements Management is the central hub joined to every phase; and that Phase H
(Architecture Change Management) loops back to Phase A to close the cycle. Also
confirm in plain words what Phase B hands onward to Phase C.

## What It Is (~18s)
Show "Phase B Business Architecture" as a LARGE CENTERED title with a short subtitle
beneath it, such as "Designing how the business works". Explain the words plainly:
Enterprise Architecture is just the big-picture plan of how a whole organization's
business and computer systems fit together — like a city plan, not a single building
— and the ADM (Architecture Development Method) is the step-by-step recipe TOGAF
gives you to build that plan. Phase B is the design step where you draw the Business
Architecture — the plan of how the organization works to deliver value: who does
what, the things it offers, and the steps it follows. Name the example: a small
family bakery that wants to start taking orders online.

## Phase B on the Ring (~28s)
Draw the cycle the canonical way: a ring of labelled rounded boxes running clockwise
— Phase A Architecture Vision, Phase B Business Architecture, Phase C Information
Systems Architectures, Phase D Technology Architecture, Phase E Opportunities and
Solutions, Phase F Migration Planning, Phase G Implementation Governance, and Phase H
Architecture Change Management — joined by curved ACCENT arrows that show the
clockwise flow. Place Requirements Management — keeping track of everything the
organization needs the architecture to do — as a hub in the centre joined to every
phase. Draw the Preliminary phase as a separate box outside the ring with one arrow
feeding into Phase A — the one-time setup before the cycle begins, not a step on the
ring. Close the ring with an arrow from Phase H back to Phase A. Put Phase B in
HIGHLIGHT and say it plainly: Phase B comes right after Phase A, which produced the
Architecture Vision — the short agreed picture of what you want and why — and Phase B
is the first of the design phases.

## The Job of Phase B (~22s)
Bring Phase B forward as a single HIGHLIGHT card and explain its job in plain words:
describe how the business needs to work so the Vision can come true. Show three plain
building parts as ACCENT-labelled boxes, each glossed simply: business capabilities —
what the business is able to do, like "take an order" or "bake bread"; value streams
— the chain of steps that delivers something a customer values, from order placed to
loaf handed over; and the organization — the people and roles who do the work. Note
that each of these is a Building Block — a reusable part, like a LEGO brick — so they
are the building blocks of the business.

## Inside Phase B: Inputs, Steps, Outputs (~30s)
Show a simple flow of three ACCENT-labelled bands built one at a time: Inputs, Steps,
Outputs. Inputs: the Architecture Vision from Phase A and the needs of the
stakeholders — a Stakeholder is anyone with an interest, like the owner, the bakers,
and the customers. Steps, in plain words: describe how the business works today,
describe how it should work, and find the gaps between the two. Outputs: the Target
Business Architecture (the agreed picture of how the business should work), a short
list of gaps to close, and the first pieces of the Architecture Roadmap — simply the
schedule of steps from today to the goal. All of it is written into the Architecture
Definition Document — the single document that holds the architecture descriptions.

## Baseline, Target, and the Gap (~24s)
Draw two columns of small boxes for the heart of the work. A Baseline column shows how
the bakery works today (the "before"): a walk-in counter only, orders written by hand,
cash payment. A Target column shows how it should work (the "after"): online orders, a
shared order list, a pickup point. Gloss it: the Baseline Architecture is the picture
of today, the Target Architecture is the picture of the goal. Mark the differences
between them as Gaps in DANGER — the missing pieces: no way to take an online order,
no shared order list. Comparing the two to list what is missing is called Gap
Analysis. Show one matched pair lining up with its gap flagged in DANGER. Add that each
picture is drawn from a chosen Viewpoint — the angle you look from — so a View shows
just what one audience needs, like a floor plan versus a wiring diagram of the same
building.

## One Pass for a Bakery (~36s)
Walk the bakery through Phase B with the cycle kept small in a corner, Phase B in
HIGHLIGHT. Carry in the Vision from Phase A: "take orders online and deliver fresh
bread within one year". Baseline today: customers queue at the counter, a baker writes
each order on paper, payment is cash. Target: customers place orders online, every
order drops into one shared order list, staff pack the bread and hand it over at a
pickup point. Capabilities needed: "take an online order", "track an order", "schedule
a pickup". Value stream: from order placed to bread handed over. Gap Analysis in
DANGER: no online-order capability, no shared order list, no pickup step. Outputs in
OK: a Target Business Architecture everyone has agreed, a short gap list, and roadmap
pieces ready to hand to Phase C, the software design step. Build it step by step,
ending with the agreed line of sight in OK.

## Conclusion (~20s)
Recap in plain words: Phase B Business Architecture is the design step where you
picture how the business should work to deliver the Vision — its capabilities, value
streams, and people — then compare today (Baseline) with the goal (Target) to find the
Gaps. Remind that it sits second on the ring of Phases A through H around the
Requirements Management hub, takes the Vision in from Phase A, and hands a checked
Target Business Architecture and gap list onward to Phase C. End with the line of sight
on screen: Vision -> Today -> Target -> Gaps -> Roadmap.
