# Phase G Implementation Governance

A short tutorial on Phase G Implementation Governance, the step in TOGAF's
Architecture Development Method (ADM) that makes sure what actually gets built
matches the plan that was agreed. This is the adm_cycle teaching method: it is a
process / phase topic, so its main visual is the ADM CYCLE — a clockwise ring of
phases around a central hub, with Phase G put in focus. TOGAF is a methodology /
framework, not program code, so every scene is built from clean labelled diagrams —
rounded boxes, the ADM cycle, matrices, layered bands, spectrums, and relationship
arrows — never from source code, and never with an invented "official TOGAF icon"
(TOGAF has no standard icon set). TOGAF is full of technical terms, so every term is
explained in plain words the first time it appears — assume the viewer has never
heard them. The ADM is always drawn the canonical way: Phases A through H arranged
clockwise on a ring with Requirements Management as the central hub joined to every
phase, the Preliminary phase drawn outside the ring feeding one arrow into Phase A
(Architecture Vision), and Phase H looping back to Phase A to close the ring. The
running example is from everyday life: a small family bakery that has already planned
online ordering and is now building and rolling it out. Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT
for phase and relationship labels, HIGHLIGHT for the phase in focus, OK for a
plan-matching finished result, and DANGER for work that drifts away from the agreed
plan. The English version should be entirely in English, including the examples.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the canonical structure and terminology of Phase G
Implementation Governance against an authoritative TOGAF source (The Open Group's
TOGAF Standard) instead of recalling it from memory, so the diagram matches the
official method and not an invented one. Confirm: the exact name of Phase G
(Implementation Governance) and its position on the ring between Phase F (Migration
Planning) and Phase H (Architecture Change Management); the purpose of Phase G; its
main inputs (the Architecture Roadmap and the Implementation and Migration Plan); its
key activities (confirm scope and priorities, identify deployment resources and skills,
guide solution deployment, perform architecture compliance reviews, run the business
and IT operations, do a post-implementation review); and its main outputs (a signed
Architecture Contract, Compliance Assessments, an Implementation Governance Model, and
architecture-compliant delivered solutions). Because the ADM cycle is involved, also
confirm: the phase names and their clockwise order; that the ring is Phases A through H
only; that the Preliminary phase sits outside the ring with a single arrow into Phase A
(Architecture Vision) and is not a box on the ring; that Requirements Management is the
central hub joined to every phase; and that Phase H (Architecture Change Management)
loops back to Phase A to close the cycle.

## What It Is (~18s)
Show "Phase G Implementation Governance" as a LARGE CENTERED title, with a short
subtitle beneath it such as "Making sure what gets built matches the plan". Explain the
words plainly: Enterprise Architecture is just the big-picture plan of how a whole
organization's business and computer systems fit together — like a city plan, not a
single building. The ADM (Architecture Development Method) is the step-by-step recipe
TOGAF gives you to build that plan, run as Phases A through H. Governance, in plain
words, means keeping the work on track and accountable — like a referee making sure
everyone follows the agreed rules. So Phase G is the refereeing step. Name the example:
a small family bakery that has finished planning its online ordering and is now building
it.

## Where It Sits in the Cycle (~30s)
Draw the ADM cycle: a ring of labelled rounded boxes running clockwise — Phase A
Architecture Vision, Phase B Business Architecture, Phase C Information Systems
Architectures, Phase D Technology Architecture, Phase E Opportunities and Solutions,
Phase F Migration Planning, Phase G Implementation Governance, and Phase H Architecture
Change Management — joined by curved ACCENT arrows showing the clockwise flow. Draw the
Preliminary phase as a separate box outside the ring with one arrow feeding into Phase A
— it is the one-time setup before the cycle begins, not a step on the ring. Close the
ring with an arrow from Phase H back to Phase A. Place Requirements Management as a hub
in the centre, joined to every phase, in plain words "keeping track of what the
organization needs". Put Phase G in HIGHLIGHT, and say it plainly: the earlier phases
decide WHAT to build and plan it; Phase G is where the building actually happens and is
watched, sitting just after Phase F (the planning step) and just before Phase H (the
later-changes step).

## Why Phase G Exists (~22s)
State the purpose in one plain line: Phase G makes sure the thing that gets built really
matches the design that everyone agreed. Show two labelled boxes — a Target Architecture
in ACCENT (plain words: "the design we are aiming for, the goal blueprint") and the
Baseline Architecture in ACCENT (plain words: "where things stand today") — and explain
that without Phase G the builders can quietly drift from the agreed design. Introduce
Compliance plainly: checking that what is built matches the agreed plan, like a building
inspector checking a new wall against the approved drawings. Show one box that follows
the plan in OK and one that has drifted in DANGER, so the point of the refereeing is
clear.

## Inputs, Steps, Outputs (~30s)
Lay Phase G out as a simple flow of three ACCENT-labelled groups and explain each in
plain words. Inputs: the Architecture Roadmap (the schedule of steps from today to the
goal) and the migration plan handed over from Phase F. Steps, as a short stack of boxes:
confirm what is being delivered and in what order; line up the people and skills to do
it; guide the builders as they work; run compliance reviews to catch drift early; switch
the new way of working on; then do a post-implementation review to confirm it all holds
together. Outputs in OK: an Architecture Contract — in plain words a signed written deal
between the architects and the builders on exactly what will be delivered, like a builder's
quote you both sign — Compliance Assessments (the written verdicts of those checks), and an
Implementation Governance Model (the agreed rules for who checks the work and how often).

## What It Delivers and Connects To (~22s)
Bring the neighbours of Phase G forward as three boxes joined by ACCENT arrows. From
Phase F (Migration Planning) comes the agreed plan and roadmap that Phase G governs. Phase
G itself produces the signed Architecture Contract and the Compliance Assessments in OK.
When the work uncovers something the plan did not foresee, Phase G raises a Change Request
— in plain words a note asking for a change — in DANGER, and passes it onward to Phase H
(Architecture Change Management), the step that decides what to do about later changes.
Say it plainly: Phase G turns an approved plan into a delivered, checked result, and feeds
anything new into the next lap of the cycle.

## One Pass for a Bakery (~32s)
Walk the bakery through Phase G, the current step in HIGHLIGHT, building it up box by box.
The agreed plan from earlier phases: an online ordering page, a saved customer list, and
secure card payments, to be live within six months. The Architecture Contract: the owner
and the people building the website sign a written deal on exactly those three things.
The Implementation Governance Model: a quick check at the end of every month. A compliance
review then catches a problem — the builders chose a cheaper card-payment method that is
not the secure one agreed — show that drifted piece in DANGER, raise a Change Request, and
correct it back to the plan. The post-implementation review then confirms the live online
ordering really matches the agreed design, shown in OK. End with the finished bakery system
on screen, plan and delivery side by side and matching.

## Conclusion (~20s)
Recap in plain words: Phase G Implementation Governance is the refereeing step of the ADM
cycle — it sits between Phase F and Phase H, and it makes sure what gets built matches what
was agreed. Show the cycle once more with Phase G in HIGHLIGHT and the central Requirements
Management hub still in view. Stress that its tools are the signed Architecture Contract and
regular compliance reviews, that anything new it finds flows on to Phase H for the next lap,
and leave the line of sight on screen: Agreed Plan -> Build and Check -> Delivered Result.
