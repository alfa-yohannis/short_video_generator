# Phase E Opportunities and Solutions

A short tutorial on Phase E, Opportunities and Solutions, one step of the
Architecture Development Method (ADM) at the heart of the TOGAF framework. TOGAF is
a widely used framework — a shared recipe — for Enterprise Architecture, which just
means the big-picture plan of how a whole organization's business and computer
systems fit together, like a city plan rather than a single building. This is the
adm_cycle teaching method: Phase E is a process topic, so the main visual is the ADM
CYCLE — a clockwise ring of phases around a central hub — with Phase E held in focus,
then its inputs, parts, and outputs walked through one at a time. TOGAF is full of
technical terms, so every term is explained in plain words the first time it appears,
assuming the viewer has never heard it, ideally with an everyday comparison. TOGAF is
a methodology, not program code, so every scene is built from clean labelled diagrams
— rounded boxes, the cycle, matrices, layered bands, spectrums, and relationship
arrows — never source code, and never with an invented "official TOGAF icon" (TOGAF
has no standard icon set). Whenever the ADM appears it is drawn the canonical way:
Phases A through H clockwise on a ring with Requirements Management as the central hub
joined to every phase, the Preliminary phase outside the ring feeding one arrow into
Phase A (Architecture Vision), and Phase H looping back to Phase A to close the ring.
The running example is from everyday life: a small family bakery that wants to start
taking orders online. Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT for phase and relationship
labels, HIGHLIGHT for the part in focus, OK for a finished result, and DANGER for a
gap to be closed. The English version should be entirely in English, including the
examples.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the canonical structure and terminology of Phase E
against an authoritative TOGAF source (The Open Group's TOGAF Standard) instead of
recalling it from memory, so the diagram matches the official method and not an
invented one. Confirm for this topic: that Phase E is named "Opportunities and
Solutions" and sits clockwise between Phase D (Technology Architecture) and Phase F
(Migration Planning); that it is the first phase directly concerned with
implementation; that its core activities are consolidating the gap analysis results
from Phases B, C, and D, identifying Solution Building Blocks, grouping changes into
Work Packages, identifying Transition Architectures, and producing the initial
Architecture Roadmap and the outline Implementation and Migration Plan; that its key
deliverables are the Architecture Roadmap, the Transition Architectures, the outline
Implementation and Migration Plan, the Solution Building Blocks, and the consolidated
Capability Assessment; and the plain-words difference between an Architecture Building
Block (a described capability) and a Solution Building Block (the concrete thing that
delivers it), and what a Transition Architecture is. Whenever the ADM cycle is drawn,
also confirm: the phase names and their clockwise order; that the ring is Phases A
through H only; that the Preliminary phase sits outside the ring with a single arrow
into Phase A (Architecture Vision) and is not a box on the ring; that Requirements
Management is the central hub joined to every phase; and that Phase H (Architecture
Change Management) loops back to Phase A to close the cycle.

## What It Is (~16s)
Show "Phase E Opportunities and Solutions" as a LARGE CENTERED title in PRIMARY, with
a short subtitle beneath it such as "From gaps to a staged plan of work". Explain the
words plainly: the ADM is TOGAF's step-by-step recipe for building the big-picture
plan, and Phase E is the step where you stop describing the future and start working
out HOW to reach it — gathering every needed change and grouping it into do-able
chunks of work. Name the example: a small family bakery that wants to start taking
orders online.

## Where Phase E Sits in the Cycle (~30s)
Draw the ADM cycle the canonical way: a ring of labelled rounded boxes running
clockwise — Phase A Architecture Vision (the agreed short picture of the goal), Phase
B Business Architecture (how the business works), Phase C Information Systems
Architectures (the software and data), Phase D Technology Architecture (the computers
underneath), Phase E Opportunities and Solutions, Phase F Migration Planning, Phase G
Implementation Governance, and Phase H Architecture Change Management — joined by
curved ACCENT arrows showing the clockwise flow. Draw the Preliminary phase as a
separate box outside the ring with one arrow into Phase A — the one-time setup before
the cycle begins, not a step on the ring. Close the ring with an arrow from Phase H
back to Phase A. Place Requirements Management as a hub in the centre — in plain words,
keeping track of everything the organization needs — joined to every phase. HIGHLIGHT
Phase E and say plainly: it comes right after the three design phases B, C, and D, and
right before Phase F, so it is the turning point from "what we want" to "how we build it".

## Why Phase E Exists (~22s)
State the purpose in plain words: Phase E is the FIRST phase about doing, not just
describing. Phases B, C, and D each found Gaps — gloss a Gap as the difference between
where you are now (the Baseline Architecture — a picture of how things work today) and
where you want to be (the Target Architecture — a picture of how you want things to
work). Show three scattered gap cards in DANGER, one from each of Phases B, C, and D,
flowing into a single Phase E box in HIGHLIGHT. Out of it comes one tidy schedule in
OK. Say it simply: Phase E collects all those scattered gaps and turns them into one
realistic, grouped plan of work.

## The Key Parts (~32s)
Show the parts Phase E produces as ACCENT-labelled boxes, HIGHLIGHTing each in turn,
each with a quick bakery example. A Solution Building Block — the actual product or
part that does the job, like a real LEGO brick you can buy, as opposed to just a
drawing of one (an Architecture Building Block describes a needed capability; a
Solution Building Block is the concrete thing that delivers it) — for example a real
online ordering website. A Work Package — a bundle of related changes grouped so one
team can deliver them together, like one item on a to-do list — for example the whole
"take payments" bundle. A Transition Architecture — a usable in-between version of the
plan, a stepping stone between today and the final goal, like moving into a house one
renovated room at a time — for example a simple email order form before the full
website. An Architecture Roadmap — simply the schedule that lays the work packages and
stepping stones out over time. Show the finished roadmap in OK.

## Inputs, Outputs, and Neighbours (~24s)
Draw the flow through the Phase E box held in HIGHLIGHT. Feeding in with ACCENT arrows:
the Architecture Vision (the agreed goal from Phase A) and the consolidated Gap lists
from Phases B, C, and D. Coming out in OK: the initial Architecture Roadmap, the
Transition Architectures, and the outline Implementation and Migration Plan — gloss
this as the first draft of the project plan for the move. Draw one ACCENT arrow handing
these to Phase F Migration Planning — the next phase, which puts firm dates and
priorities on the roadmap. Say it plainly: Phase E does the big chunking, Phase F does
the detailed scheduling.

## One Pass for the Bakery (~34s)
Walk the bakery through Phase E, the current step in HIGHLIGHT. Start with the gaps
found earlier, shown in DANGER: no website to take orders, no list of customers, and
no way to take card payments online. Now Phase E. Pick the Solution Building Blocks —
the real things: an online ordering website, a simple customer list, and an online
card-payment service. Group them into Work Packages: an "online ordering" bundle, a
"customer list" bundle, and a "payments" bundle. Identify the Transition Architectures
— the stepping stones: first a plain order form sent by email, then the full ordering
website, with the bakery still serving customers at each step. Lay the bundles and
stepping stones onto the Architecture Roadmap across a few months. Build it step by
step, ending with the tidy staged roadmap in OK.

## Conclusion (~18s)
Recap in plain words: Phase E is the bridge of the ADM cycle — the crossing point
where the described future from Phases B, C, and D becomes a grouped, staged plan.
Stress that it bundles concrete Solution Building Blocks into Work Packages, marks
usable stepping-stone Transition Architectures, lays them on an Architecture Roadmap,
and hands the lot to Phase F. End with the line of sight on screen, in OK:
Gaps -> Building Blocks -> Work Packages -> Transition Architectures -> Roadmap.
