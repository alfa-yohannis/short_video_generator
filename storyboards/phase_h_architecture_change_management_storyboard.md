# Phase H Architecture Change Management

A short tutorial on Phase H Architecture Change Management, the final phase of the
Architecture Development Method (ADM) — the step-by-step recipe at the heart of the
TOGAF framework. This is the adm_cycle teaching method: a process topic, so its main
visual is the ADM CYCLE — a clockwise ring of phases around a central hub — with
Phase H put in focus. TOGAF is a methodology, not program code, so every scene is
built from clean labelled diagrams — rounded boxes, the cycle, simple scales, and
relationship arrows — never source code, and never with an invented "official TOGAF
icon" (TOGAF has no standard icon set). TOGAF is full of technical terms, so every
term is explained in plain words the first time it appears — assume the viewer has
never heard them. The ADM is always drawn the canonical way: Phases A through H
arranged clockwise on a ring with Requirements Management as the central hub joined
to every phase, the Preliminary phase drawn outside the ring feeding one arrow into
Phase A (Architecture Vision), and Phase H looping back to Phase A to close the ring. The running example is from everyday life: a
small family bakery that has already launched online ordering and now needs to keep
it healthy as things change. Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT for phase and
relationship labels, HIGHLIGHT for the phase in focus, OK for a finished result, and
DANGER for a problem or a plan drifting out of date. The English version should be
entirely in English, including the examples.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the canonical ADM structure and Phase H's role against
an authoritative TOGAF source (The Open Group's TOGAF Standard) rather than recalling it
from memory, so the cycle matches the official method. Confirm: the ring is Phases A
through H only; the Preliminary phase sits outside the ring with a single arrow into
Phase A (Architecture Vision) and is not a box on the ring; Requirements Management is
the central hub joined to every phase; and Phase H (Architecture Change Management)
loops back to Phase A to close the cycle. Also confirm Phase H's real inputs, steps, and
outputs, and the three official classes of change (simplification, incremental,
re-architecting) and what each one triggers.

## What It Is (~18s)
Show "Phase H Architecture Change Management" as a LARGE CENTERED title with a short
subtitle, such as "Keeping the plan alive after it goes live". Explain the words
plainly: Enterprise Architecture is just the big-picture plan of how a whole
organization's business and computer systems fit together — like a city plan, not a
single building — and the ADM is the step-by-step method TOGAF gives you to build and
update that plan. Phase H is the very last step: once the new design is built and
running, this is where you keep watch and manage any change to it. Name the example: a
small family bakery whose online ordering is already live, now wanting to keep it
running smoothly as the world changes.

## Where Phase H Sits (~26s)
Draw the cycle: a ring of labelled rounded boxes running clockwise — Phase A
Architecture Vision, Phase B Business Architecture, Phase C Information Systems
Architectures, Phase D Technology Architecture, Phase E Opportunities and Solutions,
Phase F Migration Planning, Phase G Implementation Governance, and Phase H
Architecture Change Management — joined by curved ACCENT arrows that show the
clockwise flow. Draw the Preliminary phase as a separate box outside the ring with
one arrow feeding into Phase A — the one-time setup before the cycle begins, not a
step on the ring. Place Requirements Management as a hub in the centre, joined to
every phase. Put Phase H in HIGHLIGHT, and draw its closing arrow back to Phase A
Architecture Vision. Say it plainly: Phase H is the last step on the ring — the
design is already built and live, and this step keeps an eye on it and, whenever
something needs to change, sends the work back round to Phase A to start a fresh lap.

## Why Phase H Exists (~18s)
Show the purpose with two contrasting ACCENT-labelled boxes. The world never holds
still: prices rise, customers ask for new things, and technology ages. Explain
plainly that without a routine for change, even a carefully built plan slowly drifts
out of date and stops being worth its cost — show that drift in DANGER. Phase H is
that routine: the always-on activity that keeps the architecture — the agreed plan —
current, trusted, and still useful — show that staying-useful result in OK.

## Inputs, Steps, Outputs (~30s)
Lay out three ACCENT-labelled groups, each with a one-line bakery example. Inputs:
the change requests that arrive — a new business need, a technology problem, or a
measurement showing something is underperforming — together with the Architecture
Repository, in plain words the shelf where every agreed plan and reusable part is
kept. Steps, as a short plain-word list: keep watch with monitoring tools, weigh up
each change for its value and its risk, sort the change by how big it is, then run it
through Governance — the agreed approval routine, like getting the right people to
sign off. Outputs: either a small update to the plan, or a brand-new Request for
Architecture Work — plainly, a written request to start the whole cycle over for a
bigger change. Show the outputs in OK.

## The Three Kinds of Change (~24s)
Show a simple three-rung scale, each rung ACCENT-labelled and growing in size.
Simplification change — a tiny tidy-up handled on the spot, like flipping a setting;
smallest, shown in OK. Incremental change — a moderate addition that reuses most of
what already exists, so it loops through only part of the cycle. Re-architecting
change — a big change that touches the whole design and restarts the full ADM cycle
from Phase A; largest, shown in DANGER. Say it plainly: Phase H's main job is to
decide which of these three a change is, because that decides how much work it sets
off.

## One Change at the Bakery (~34s)
Walk the bakery through one real change, Phase H in HIGHLIGHT. The online ordering is
live and busy. Two changes arrive: the card-payment company raises its fees, and
customers keep asking for a points-and-rewards card. Step through it. Monitoring shows
payment costs climbing (DANGER) and a steady stream of reward requests. Weigh value
and risk. Sort each one: swapping to a cheaper card-payment service reuses almost
everything, so it is an incremental change; adding a whole rewards scheme tied to a
new customer profile is far bigger, a re-architecting change that issues a new Request
for Architecture Work and loops back to Phase A. Run both through Governance — the
bakery owner signs off. Draw the loop arrow from Phase H back round to Phase A on the
ring, and show the agreed decision in OK.

## Conclusion (~20s)
Recap in plain words: Phase H is the final phase of the ADM ring — the always-on watch
that keeps a finished plan current. It takes in changes, weighs them, sorts them into
small, medium, or big, and then either makes a quick update or starts the whole cycle
again. Stress that the ADM is repeatable, and that Phase H is exactly what feeds the
next lap. End with the line of sight on screen:
Watch -> Weigh -> Sort -> Update or Restart.
