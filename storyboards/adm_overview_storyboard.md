# ADM Overview

A short tutorial on the Architecture Development Method (ADM), the engine at the
centre of the TOGAF framework. TOGAF is a methodology — a structured way of working,
not a piece of software — so it is taught entirely with clean labelled diagrams:
rounded boxes, the ADM cycle ring, matrices, layered bands, generic-to-specific
spectrums, and relationship maps with labelled arrows — never as source code, and
never with an invented "official TOGAF icon", because TOGAF has no standard icon set.
This is the adm_cycle teaching method, chosen because ADM is a process topic, so its
main visual is the ADM CYCLE — a clockwise ring of phases turning around a central
hub. TOGAF is full of technical words an ordinary viewer has never heard, so every
term is glossed in plain language the first time it appears — assume the viewer is
hearing it for the first time. The ADM is always drawn the canonical way: Phases A
through H clockwise on a ring, Requirements Management as the central hub joined to
every phase, the Preliminary phase drawn outside the ring feeding one arrow into
Phase A (Architecture Vision), and Phase H looping back to Phase A to close the ring.
The running example is from everyday life: a small family bakery that wants to start
taking orders online. Use PRIMARY for titles, ACCENT for phase and relationship
labels, HIGHLIGHT for the phase in focus, OK for a finished result, and DANGER for a
gap still to be closed. The English version is entirely in English, including the
example.

# Preparation
Before drawing any scene, verify the canonical ADM structure against an authoritative
TOGAF source (The Open Group's TOGAF Standard) instead of recalling it from memory, so
the diagram matches the official method rather than an invented one. Confirm the eight
phase names and their exact clockwise order; that the ring is Phases A through H only;
that the Preliminary phase sits outside the ring with a single arrow into Phase A
(Architecture Vision) and is never drawn as a box on the ring; that Requirements
Management is the central hub joined to every phase; and that Phase H (Architecture
Change Management) loops back to Phase A to close the cycle. Also confirm, in plain
words, what each phase delivers and that the cycle is meant to be repeated.

## What It Is (~18s)
Show "ADM Overview" as a LARGE CENTERED title with a short subtitle directly under it,
such as "TOGAF's step-by-step recipe". Explain the words plainly: Enterprise
Architecture is just the big-picture plan of how a whole organization's business and
computer systems fit together — like a city plan for a whole town, not the blueprint
of one building. The ADM (Architecture Development Method) is the step-by-step recipe
TOGAF gives you to build that plan and keep it up to date. Name the running example: a
small family bakery that wants to start taking orders online.

## The ADM Cycle (~32s)
Draw the cycle as a ring of labelled rounded boxes running clockwise — Phase A
Architecture Vision, Phase B Business Architecture, Phase C Information Systems
Architectures, Phase D Technology Architecture, Phase E Opportunities and Solutions,
Phase F Migration Planning, Phase G Implementation Governance, and Phase H Architecture
Change Management — joined by curved ACCENT arrows showing the clockwise flow. Draw the
Preliminary phase as a separate box outside the ring with one arrow feeding into Phase
A — it is the one-time setup done before the cycle ever turns, not a step on the ring.
Close the ring with an arrow from Phase H back to Phase A. Say it plainly: each box on
the ring is one step, you go round in order, and the centre keeps everyone's needs in
view. Place Requirements Management as a hub in the centre, joined to every phase.
HIGHLIGHT each phase name in turn as it is introduced.

## What the Phases Deliver (~30s)
Group the phases by intent, each ACCENT-labelled, and explain each result plainly.
Preliminary sets up the team and the ground rules. Phase A produces an Architecture
Vision — a short agreed picture of what you want and why, like a quick sketch of a
finished room before a renovation. Phases B, C, and D describe the Target Architecture
— where you want to be — for the business, the information and software, and the
technology underneath, and compare it with the Baseline Architecture — where you are
today. The difference between today and the goal is a Gap — the missing pieces you
still need; show each Gap in DANGER. Phases E and F turn the gaps into an Architecture
Roadmap — simply the schedule of steps from today to the goal. Phases G and H oversee
the building work and fold in later changes. Show each finished result in OK.

## Requirements at the Hub (~20s)
Bring the centre forward: Requirements Management — in plain words, keeping track of
everything the organization needs the architecture to do. Explain it is not a step you
finish and leave behind, but an always-on activity: every phase adds new needs and
reads the current ones, so when something changes the change ripples through every
phase consistently. Draw two-way ACCENT arrows between the hub and a couple of phases
on the ring, and keep the hub in HIGHLIGHT.

## How the Phases Hand Off Work (~18s)
Show a simple relationship map of the chain of outputs that links the phases, so the
ring is more than a list. A Deliverable is a finished, signed-off output one phase
hands to the next, like passing a baton in a relay. A Building Block is a reusable part
of the design you can use again and again, like a LEGO brick — for the bakery, a single
"online payment" piece reused everywhere money changes hands. Governance, named in
Phase G, simply means someone oversees the building work to keep it on track and on the
rules, like a referee. Draw ACCENT arrows: Vision -> Target design with its Gaps in
DANGER -> Roadmap -> overseen Delivery.

## One Pass for a Bakery (~36s)
Walk the bakery through a single lap of the cycle, the current phase in HIGHLIGHT.
Preliminary: agree the ground rules and set up a small team — the owner, the baker, and
a helper who is good with computers. Phase A: the vision — "let customers order cakes
and bread online and collect or get them delivered within one year". Phase B: how the
business will work — a new way to take online orders and a simple delivery routine.
Phase C: the software — an online ordering page and a customer list. Phase D: the
technology underneath — renting computing space online (the cloud) and a way to accept
card payments. Phases E and F: a roadmap of small, affordable steps. Phases G and H:
keep the rollout on track and fold in what is learned from busy weekends. Build it step
by step, ending with the finished line of sight in OK.

## Conclusion (~22s)
Recap in plain words: the ADM is a repeating cycle — Phases A through H turning around a
Requirements Management hub, set up once by the Preliminary phase outside the ring —
that turns goals into a checked, delivered design. Stress that it is repeatable: when
the bakery later adds, say, a loyalty card, you simply go round again. End with the line
of sight on screen, each step in OK: Vision -> Target Design -> Roadmap -> Delivery.
